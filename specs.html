Tech Specs

Since their introduction in the early 1990s, usage of bioplastics have had a profound industry on the packaging industry. As we as a society become more aware of the implications of plastic waste and its near millennia time delay for decomposition, we strive to focus on these as alternatives in the disposable market.

According to the Biodegradable Product Institute (BPI), a biodegradable product is one that under the right conditions the microbes in the environment can break down the material and use it as a food source. Unfortunately, this limits the eco-friendliness of many of these materials as without the proper resources and infrastructure, they cause more pollution by not properly disintegrating. Bioplastic is often impregnated with other materials such as gauze to make it contain its industrial strength. This makes it further difficult to break down.

EdiBags is primarily composed of starch.

Starch contains the compound amylose, which allows it to form a strong film when combined with water and other ingredients. Adding fruit or vegetable strengthens this combination further due to the long chain carbohydrates present within them (cellulose and pectin) which provide a strong structural matrix in addition to that provided by starch. When they are consumed, there is a reduction in plastic pollution. However, even when they are disposed of, they decompose similar to a fruit peel; they take a similar amount of time and do not need a very specific environment.
